REFLECTION

During our extension activities, we encountered several setbacks that tested our patience, creativity, and perseverance. One of the main challenges was getting approval from the School Head. When we proposed a program for the Love Month celebration, our Cooperating Teachers were more hesitant than enthusiastic. While they appreciated the initiative, they expressed uncertainty, saying we could try proposing it to the School Head—but not to expect much. As it turned out, the School Head was not particularly fond of programs. Though he eventually agreed, he imposed strict conditions: the event had to be held after class dismissal and completed within just one hour.

At first, this was disheartening. Personally, as the assigned Master of Ceremony, I felt a great deal of pressure figuring out how to fit all the segments into such a short time. Despite our doubts, we decided to proceed and make the most of the opportunity.

Another issue that caught us off guard was the prohibition on collecting money from students. We initially planned to sell sticky notes for the Freedom Wall at one peso each, but the School Head made it clear that selling was not allowed. Instead, he suggested placing a donation box so students could contribute voluntarily. We adjusted our plans accordingly.

Budget constraints were another major hurdle. For our first extension activity, the school provided only four pieces of cartolina. Obviously, that wasn’t enough to complete the entire project, which is a Freedom Wall, and two photobooth frames. Even basic supplies like glue, tape, and boxes were not available. When I went to the Principal’s Office to request a roll of 2-inch packing tape, the Administrative Officer told me they didn’t even have scotch tape. I was genuinely surprised. I asked again just to be sure, and she confirmed it—they didn’t have any school supplies for such needs.

Frustrated but not defeated, I returned to my co-interns and shared what had happened. We all sighed and immediately began pooling our personal materials. I got my double-sided tape from the library and told my CT what had happened. She wasn't surprised. I learned that even teachers don’t always receive basic office supplies. Fortunately, she had a few glue sticks she kindly let us use. The Science teacher and canteen staff also donated some boxes. Additional materials like colored paper and extra cartolina came from our fellow interns. It was truly a group effort.

Thankfully, our second extension activity—painting the social hall—went much more smoothly. We were pleasantly surprised when the school provided snacks and lunch, since we initially planned to bring packed meals. The only hiccup we encountered was with the rice, which had been stored in the Principal’s Office for months and was infested with weevils. Disgusted but with no other option, I carefully cleaned the rice until it looked safe enough to cook and eat. Despite the situation, we still shared the meal with gratitude.

Participating in the extension activities during my internship—the Love Month celebration (featuring a photobooth, freedom wall, and flower sale) and the repainting of the social hall—taught me valuable lessons not only as a future teacher, but also as a team player and active member of a school community. One of the most powerful realizations I had was the importance of unity. None of our plans would have succeeded without the collaboration and shared commitment of my co-interns and the students. Each of us had a role, and together we turned simple ideas into meaningful experiences.

Another lesson I took to heart was learning how to navigate the challenge of limited resources. Even when materials and funds were scarce, we found creative ways to push through—whether through personal contributions, upcycling, or asking for support. This experience empowered me as a teacher-in-training. It reminded me that teaching extends beyond classroom walls—it’s about problem-solving, being resourceful, and making the best of what’s available. I learned to appreciate the spirit of service, the strength in simplicity, and the joy of turning challenges into victories.

In the end, what truly mattered was not the size of our budget, the perfection of our plans, or the smoothness of our execution—but the heart, passion, and purpose we poured into everything we did. These experiences reminded me that even with limited resources, meaningful impact is still possible when we work together with sincerity and creativity.
